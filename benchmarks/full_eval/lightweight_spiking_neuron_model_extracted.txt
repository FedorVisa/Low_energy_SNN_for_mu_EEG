 A B S T R A C T
Spiking neural networks (SNNs) aim to simulate the human brain neural network, using sparse spike event 
streams for effective and energy-efficient spatio-temporal signal processing. This paper proposes a lightweight 
SNN model for electroencephalogram (EEG) based motor imagery (MI) classification, a classical brain–
computer interface paradigm. The model has three desirable characteristics: (1) it has a brain-inspired 
architecture; (2) it is energy efficient; and, (3) it is dataset agnostic. Within-subject and cross-subject 
experiments on three public datasets demonstrated the superiority of our SNN model over four classical 
convolutional neural network based models in EEG based MI classification.
1. Introduction
Electroencephalogram (EEG) has been widely used in brain-
computer interfaces (BCIs) to decode human mental states. Motor 
imagery (MI) is a classic BCI paradigm, which involves imagining 
performing a movement without actually executing it. It has broad 
applications in healthcare, such as neurological rehabilitation (López-
Larraz et al., 2018), brain-controlled wheelchairs (Fernández-Rodríguez 
et al., 2016), and brain-controlled robotic arms (Cheng et al., 2025).
In recent years, EEG-based MI classification has been extensively 
studied (Hameed et al., 2025). The algorithms can be broadly catego-
rized into traditional machine learning approaches and deep learning 
approaches. The former use hand-crafted EEG features in traditional 
(shallow) classifiers (Ang et al., 2012), whereas the latter use deep 
neural networks to learn the feature extractor and classifier simulta-
neously in an end-to-end manner (Lawhern et al., 2018; Mane et al., 
2021; Schirrmeister et al., 2017).
This paper belongs to the second category. However, instead of 
using (traditional) artificial neural networks (ANNs), we use spiking 
neural networks (SNNs), which have been proven in theory and practice 
to be able to better capture the temporal–spatial nature of EEGs with 
low energy consumption (Kugele et al., 2020; Shan et al., 2025; Shi 
et al., 2025; Wu et al., 2019; Yu et al., 2022). More specifically, we 
propose a lightweight end-to-end SNN model based on spatial–temporal 
convolution for EEG-based MI classification. Its main advantages are:

1. Brain-inspired. The model emulates human brain activity by in-

high performance across various datasets using a single set of

2.1. MI classification
EEG-based MI classification algorithms can be broadly categorized 
into traditional machine learning approaches and deep learning ap-
proaches (Hameed et al., 2025).
Traditional approaches may extract temporal features such as mean 
and variance, spectral features such as power spectral density and 
wavelets, and/or spatial features such as filter bank common spatial 
patterns (Ang et al., 2012), and then feed them into traditional (shal-
low) classifiers, such as linear discriminant analysis and support vector 
machine, for classification.
Deep learning approaches usually use end-to-end ANNs, such as 
CNNs and long short-term memory (LSTM) networks, for simultaneous 
feature extraction and classification. Representative approaches include 
ShallowNet (Schirrmeister et al., 2017), DeepNet (Schirrmeister et al., 
2017), EEGNet (Lawhern et al., 2018) and FBCNet (Mane et al., 2021).
Although ANNs demonstrate promising performance, their high 
energy consumption constrains the deployment on portable devices 
and the applications to EEG-based BCIs (Kartsch et al., 2019). In 
contrast, SNNs, which use spiking neurons representing information 
across multiple timescales, may perform better in non-stationary signal 
classification with reduced energy consumption (Deng et al., 2020; 
Tang et al., 2021).
2.2. Spiking neural network
Many spiking neuron models have been proposed such as LIF 
neuron (Fang et al., 2021), exponential integrate-and-fire (EIF) neu-
ron (Fourcaud-Trocmé et al., 2003), quadratic integrate-and-fire (QIF) 
neuron (Latham et al., 2000), Izhikevich neuron (Izhikevich, 2004) 
and neuromorphic computing-specific neurons (Chakraborty et al., 
2018; Xiao et al., 2025). Among them, the LIF neuron can be easily 
implemented and has fewer floating-point operations (FLOPs), thus LIF 
neuron and its variants are the most popular in SNN algorithms (Fang 
et al., 2021; Wu et al., 2021; Yao et al., 2022).
Unlike ANNs, SNNs process data in the form of spike sequences, re-
quiring encoding the conventional analog data into spike events (Forno 
et al., 2022). Common encoding mechanisms include rate-based, tem-
poral, bio-inspired and direct approaches. Among them, direct encoding 
sets spike encoding as the input layer of the neural network (Fang et al., 
2021), having the lowest encoding costs.
Due to the non-differentiable nature of spiking neuron models, the 
training of SNNs is also different from ANNs. There are generally three 
categories of methods (Tavanaei et al., 2019): ANN-to-SNN conversion, 
direct training, and biologically plausible training. Among them, di-
rect training replaces the non-differentiable firing function in spiking 
neurons with a surrogate function to solve non-differentiable problem 
when performing backpropagation. It is easy to implement and usually 
gets better results.
In this paper, we use the LIF neuron, direct encoding mechanism 
and direct training method.
2.3. SNN-based MI classification
SNNs simulate real neurons in the human brain and use asyn-
chronous spike event streams for information transmission, which can 
better process time series with reduced energy consumption (Karami-
manesh et al., 2025; Lobo et al., 2020; Tavanaei et al., 2019).
Manually extracted features can be used in SNNs for MI classifica-
tion.Virgilio et al. (2020) combined power spectral density and wavelet 
transform with Izhikevich neurons for binary MI classification, demon-
strating the advantages of SNNs in processing time information. Wang 
et al. (2020) used common spatial pattern and filter bank common 
spatial pattern for EEG feature extraction, and then Izhikevich neu-
rons for online multi-classification MI classification. Wu et al. (2023) 
extracted spatio-temporal features of EEGs, then performed binary MI 
classification using a dynamic evolving SNN.
End-to-end SNNs have also been proposed for MI classification. Li 
et al. (2022) proposed an end-to-end LSTM-based SNN model with 
Bens Spiker algorithm for transfer learning in MI classification. Kumar 
et al. (2022) used multi-layer convolution to encode spikes, i.e., tem-
poral convolution and recurrent form of spiking neurons to capture 
the spatio-temporal information. Gong et al. (2023) designed a novel 
SNN model with adaptive graph convolution and LSTM, considering 
the intrinsic spatial topology among different EEG channels and the 
temporal dependencies of spikes. Liao et al. (2025) proposed an ANN-
to-SNN conversion method for MI classification that reduces conversion 
errors and improves parameter efficiency via a fully convolutional 
structure.
Currently, end-to-end SNNs may achieve better performance; how-
ever, they use dataset-specific hyperparameters, significantly reducing 
their practicality.

4. Experiments and results
This section introduces the experiment datasets and results to 
demonstrate the effectiveness and biological interpretability of our 
proposed SNN model. The code will be available at https://github.com/
Zhr1110/SnnForMI after this paper is accepted.
4.1. Datasets
Three publicly available MI datasets, summarized in Table  2, were 
used in our experiments.
The first (MI1) was BCI Competition IV Dataset 2a (Brunner et al., 
2008), which contains 22-channel EEG signals from four classes (left 
hand, right hand, feet, and tongue). It consists of data from two 
sessions, one for training and the other for test.
The second (MI2) (Steyrl et al., 2016) contains 15-channel EEG 
signals from two classes (right hand, and feet). It has been partitioned 
into training and test sets.
The last (MI3) was the Weibo2014 dataset (Yi et al., 2014), which 
contains 60-channel EEG signals from six classes (left hand, right hand, 
feet, both hands, left hand combined with right foot, and right hand 
combined with left foot). The first half was used for training, and the 
second half for test.
The same preprocessing steps were used for all three datasets: de-
trending, 8–30 Hz bandpass filtering, and downsampling to 250 Hz. 
Each trial contained 4s MI data. The last 1/6 training data were 
reserved as the validation set.
4.2. Implementation details
We implemented our SNN model using the PyTorch-based Spik-
ingjelly toolkit (Fang et al., 2023). We used the two-stage training 
strategy as in Mane et al. (2021), Schirrmeister et al. (2017), which 
was proposed to maximize the utilization of EEG training data while 
reducing overfitting. Additionally, it fixes the batch size at 16 and 
the learning rate at 0.001, eliminating the need for hyperparameter 
adjusting in training. In the first stage, the maximum number of epochs 
was set to 1500, and Adam optimizer with early stopping (patience 
200) on the validation set was used. In the second stage, the model 
was trained by combined the training and validation sets and for at 
most 600 epochs. The second stage training was stopped when its loss 
reduced below that of the first stage. The experiments were repeated 8 
times, and the means are reported.

4.3. Classification results
Our SNN model was compared with four CNN models (ShallowNet
Schirrmeister et al., 2017, DeepNet Schirrmeister et al., 2017, EEG-
Net Lawhern et al., 2018, and FBCNet Mane et al., 2021) in both 
within-subject and cross-subject classifications. For FBCNet, we inde-
pendently processed the data from each frequency band as described 
in the original paper. Notably, most existing end-to-end SNNs are not 
open source (Gong et al., 2023; Li et al., 2022), and all require extensive 
hyperparameter tuning for each new dataset (Gong et al., 2023; Kumar 
et al., 2022; Li et al., 2022), hindering the reproducibility of their 
reported results. Consequently, these models were excluded from our 
experimental comparisons.
Table  3 summarizes the within-subject classification results on the 
three datasets. Our SNN model achieved the best performance on MI1 
and MI2, and second best on MI3. Compared with the four classical 
CNN based models, the average classification accuracy increased at 
least 1.93%.
Table  4 shows the leave-one-subject-out cross-subject classifica-
tion results. We conducted experiments with and without Euclidean 
alignment (EA) (He & Wu, 2020), which has demonstrated promising 
performance in transfer learning (Wu et al., 2022). Our proposed SNN 
model outperformed the other four CNN-based models, and EA can be 
used to further improve its performance.

4.5. Biological rationality
Two experiments were performed to examine the spatial and tem-
poral biological rationality of the SNN model.
In the first experiment, the EEG signal magnitude of each channel 
was gradually increased from 0.9 to 1.1 times of the original signal, 
and the correlation of EEG signal magnitude with the classification 
performance was computed. A positive correlation indicates that an 
increase in the EEG signal amplitude facilitates classification, and vice 
versa. The results are shown in Fig.  1. Reduced noise amplitude in the 
right (left) hemisphere facilitated left-hand (right-hand) classification, 
aligning with the known spatial mechanisms of MI (Hamel-Thibault 
et al., 2018).
In the second experiment, we masked signals at various intervals to 
evaluate their temporal impact on the classification performance. Fig.  2 
shows the results. Generally, a higher-power segment had larger impact 
on the SNN classification performance, which is reasonable.
In summary, these two experiments confirmed that our SNN model 
aligns with the spatial and temporal physiological mechanisms of MI.

4.6. Ablation studies
Within-subject ablation studies were performed to verify the effec-
tiveness of spatial and temporal convolution and LIF neuron in our 
model.
The effect of spatial and temporal convolution. We removed 
the spatial convolution (SC) layer and temporal convolution (TC) layer 
individually and simultaneously to evaluate their effects. Table  7 shows 
the results. Using both together achieved the best performance. Al-
though both convolutions are essential, spatial convolution, which is 
responsible for channel restructuring and spatial resolution expansion, 
seemed more important.
The effect of LIF neuron. Identity function and commonly used 
spiking neurons such as integrate-and-fire (IF), Izhikevich, EIF, and QIF 
neurons were used to replace the LIF neuron in our model to study its 
effect. Table  8 shows the results. The LIF neuron performed best and 
has lower computational complexity, so overall it is the best choice.
4.7. Visualization of spatial and temporal convolution
The spatial convolutional layer improves the spatial resolution of 
EEG signals and facilitates the integration of information from different 
brain regions. Meanwhile, the temporal convolutional layer acts as a 
time-domain filter, with the help of its kernel values.
Fig.  3 shows the spatial convolutional weights and the spectrum of 
temporal convolutional weights corresponding to the first 10 output 
channels of 𝐶out , using Subject 3 from MI1 as an example. Each output 
channel focuses on distinct brain regions and aligns with different 
filters, such as low-pass filters (𝐶6 and 𝐶10) and band-pass filters (𝐶1, 
𝐶2, and 𝐶3).
By integrating spatial and temporal information, spatial and tem-
poral convolutional layers enhance the spatial comprehension of EEG 
signals while selectively isolating different frequency components, fa-
cilitating the extraction of more informative signals for subsequent 
processing.

The LIF neuron transforms the signals processed by the spatial and 
temporal convolutional layers into sparse spike sequences, to obtain 
rate-based features.
Fig.  4 shows 𝑡-SNE visualizations of data before and after the LIF 
neuron for some representative users. Clearly, the feature distribu-
tions of different classes become more separated after the LIF neuron, 
facilitating classification.
5. Conclusions
This paper has proposed a lightweight SNN model for EEG-based 
MI classification. It employs a spatial convolutional layer to restructure 
EEGs, and a temporal convolutional layer to perform filtering. Next, 
a trainable LIF neuron adaptively encodes spikes, capturing spatial 
and temporal information. Finally, a rate-based decoder and a fully 
connected layer are utilized for classification.
Compared with four CNN models, our proposed SNN model per-
formed the best in both within-subject and cross-subject MI classifi-
cations on three public datasets, with fewer parameters, lower energy 
consumption, and better biological interpretability. Compared with ex-
isting SNNs, our model eliminates the need for hyperparameter tuning 
on different datasets, making it more convenient to use.
While the proposed model demonstrates promising performance, 
several limitations remain:
• The evaluation was conducted solely on publicly available data-
sets, leaving its generalizability to real-world scenarios unveri-
fied.
• Despite its lightweight design, the model may face increased 
computational demands with high-channel-density EEGs.
• The robustness and generalizability across heterogeneous datasets 
have not been thoroughly investigated.
Future work will address these limitations by:
• Deploying the model on neuromorphic hardware and validating 
it in real-world applications.
• Further optimizing the model architecture to improve efficiency 
and scalability for high-channel-density EEGs.
• Developing MI classification models with enhanced robustness 
and transferability across heterogeneous datasets.
